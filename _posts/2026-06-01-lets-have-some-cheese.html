I have never been good with weddings.

Still, I went.

I went because Esra, the bride, was a dear friend. We had not seen each other in years. There are friendships that wait in memory until someone sends a message beginning with a name you have missed.

I saw her first near the entrance, surrounded by the stunned glow of a person being looked at by everyone at once. She was beautiful. She looked like someone the years had carried carefully. I was happy for her.

The groom stood beside her. He was handsome in the way that makes people assume discipline. I liked him before. I liked him more that night.

She saw me. Her face opened before she moved, and then she was coming toward me with both arms out, pulling me into the white orbit of her dress. “Hakan,” she said. We held on a little too long. “I’m so happy you came,” she said. “Welcome. We saved you a seat.” She pointed toward a table near the edge of the garden, resting away from the central duties of belonging. “That one,” she said. “I am sure you are going to like everyone. Just one thing: promise me you will tell me all about the table later. You can’t know how much I would like to sit with all of you tonight.”

I knew that the table had been reserved for people who knew only one side of the couple. The table was near a low stone wall adorned with a line of white flowers. Five chairs. Four taken.

I sat in the last one, facing the sun. For the first few minutes I listened to the people around me.

The first voice I registered came from directly across the table. A man, mid-story. His sentences were precise and unhurried. Each one sounded like it had been considered for a long time before being released into isolation. One was about a ferry. Then, from somewhere on his body, a small clean sound. A click arriving from nowhere, followed by more silence, followed by the next unrelated sentence. We called him Çıtçıt before the salads arrived.

To his left sat a man in a dark suit. He had the kind of face that makes strangers assume good character. He was a yoga instructor. Someone at the table asked him the obvious question about his career choice. He smiled. “Come to a class,” he said. “Just one class. Then we talk.” He said this to three different people before the entrées, delivering the exact phrase with an unwavering patience. Yogacı.

Next to me sat a man with a kaytan mustache, curling slightly upward at the ends like a signature he had spent years perfecting. He had bicycled across the country before writing a book he described as essential. Every story felt borrowed. Çok Amaçlı.

The last chair belonged to a woman I recognized. We had met a few times, leaving an imprint on the memory. She watched the table. Every passing gesture went straight into the ear of the man beside her. Gözetmen—a lecturer at a university.

He had come with her. He was already laughing with Çıtçıt about something I had missed. Whatever she gave him he carried straight to the table, her observation already in his mouth, offered as his own. He loved the attention. Sometimes she tightened and said his name once. He was already turning back to us with the next thing she had handed him. İletken.

The wine was Kavaklıdere Yakut. The cheese came from Kars, carrying the weight of a family tradition. An aged kaşar that crumbled under the knife. Sitting at my assigned table, I was eating the best cheese of my life.

Somewhere behind us the wedding went on with a microphone someone kept testing. Out by the wall the wine had done its work, and the table had loosened.

Çıtçıt went first. He set down his glass, which I had learned to read as a beginning, and started a story about a summer boat. Each sentence was clean and certain, slowly gathering toward a deliberate ending. He took a breath for the final part—

and the waiter arrived with the cheese.

A whole board of it, featuring a hard yellow wheel with a name none of us caught. “A little more?” the waiter asked. We took some. By the time the board had gone around the table, no one remembered that Çıtçıt had stopped mid-sentence. He looked content.

Çok Amaçlı picked it up. He had sold cheese once at a market, an unprovable truth that brought him to Lake Como. He had been walking the edge of it when a storm came down off the mountains, and he had stood there soaked and laughing, explaining that good cheese was exactly like that sudden weather. It comes down off the mountain to find you. He had the table now. He opened his mouth for the end of it—

“Protein is the thing people get wrong,” said Yogacı.

Everyone turned to him. “I forgive a good cheese. I’ll walk thirty kilometers for the right one.” He let it sit. “And when it comes to your feet, maintain a healthy suspicion.” He had a story too about a weekend diving trip near Saroz. There was a cheese there he still thought about—he was just reaching the core of the memory—

“I’ve always wanted to dive,” someone said.

That was the end of Saroz. The table went under, discussing the logistics of local boats. The cheese remained abandoned in the village.

The woman by the wall had been watching the whole time. She leaned toward the man beside her with a whispered observation. He laughed and turned to the table. “She says we haven't finished a single story all night,” he announced with pride. We spent the rest of the evening proving her right.

The sun was almost gone. I liked everyone immediately. I had found the seams in their armor tonight. Naming people replaces the effort of knowing them.

The characters played their assigned roles perfectly around the table. I ate the cheese knowing it was the best of my life while the sun finished going down behind the wall.

I sat in my designated fifth chair, enjoying the weight of the evening.

Of course, I gave one to myself too—
