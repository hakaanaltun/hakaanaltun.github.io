The terminal thinned to cleaners and a long fluorescent quiet. A row of joined seats and the bag for a pillow; the boarding hour would not come.

Around three the arithmetic ran on its own. Had the grass stayed uncrossed, he would have made the ferry, and the flight; the night would already be spent on the far side, and by now he would be beside his mother at the corner in the first grey. There was the terminal instead, and the hour on the ticket; she would stand at the corner alone while he sat under the lights.

The gate came at last, a quarter to four. A seat over the wing.

The runway came up grey, a little after six, first light already on the hangars.

A taxi took the shore road into a city awake ahead of itself. The corner, when it came, had a plane tree and a shuttered grocery and, where the block had stood, a barrier folded back and a heap going dark with damp.

It was over.

His mother stood alone before the rubble, hands behind her back, in the place she had kept to the end.

She did not turn at the step behind her. She was already looking at something on the ground.

He looked first at her, then to where she was looking.

A photograph, face up. A man on a step, a child at knee height beside him, and behind them the doorframe with its first pencil line.

The first of them.

The bag stayed on his shoulder.

There was dust on the new leaves.
