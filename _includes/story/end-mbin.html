The terminal thinned to cleaners and a long fluorescent quiet. A row of joined seats, the bag for a pillow, the boarding an hour that would not come.

Around three the arithmetic ran on its own. Had the grass stayed uncrossed, the ferry would have been made, and the flight, and the night would already be spent on the far side; and by now there would be the corner in the first grey, and the mother at it, and someone beside her. Instead there was the terminal, and the hour on the ticket, and her at the corner alone while this sat under the lights.

The gate came at last, a quarter to four. A seat over the wing.

The runway came up grey, a little after six, first light already on the hangars.

A taxi took the shore road into a city awake ahead of itself. The corner, when it came, had a plane tree and a shuttered grocery and, where the block had stood, a barrier folded back and a heap going dark with damp.

It was over. It had been over for an hour.

The mother stood before the rubble, hands behind her back, alone, in the place she had kept to the end.

She did not turn at the step behind her. She was already looking at something on the ground.

The look went first to her, then to where she was looking.

A photograph, face up. A man on a step, a child at knee height beside him, and behind them the doorframe with its first pencil line.

The first of them.

The bag stayed on the shoulder.

There was dust on the new leaves.
