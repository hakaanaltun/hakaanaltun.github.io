Twenty steps onto the grass and the ferry was already lost; the rest was only how.

The teacher had come down to the chestnuts. The account was being given again, with the pages for evidence and the crying to keep time.

What I said next was not for the grass to hear. A minute, maybe less. The teacher listening, then looking past the one still crying, to where the notebook had come apart and to the hands that had held it.

The crying went on a moment out of habit, then found nothing under it, and stopped climbing. The words it had been gathering did not come.

By then the pier had its gates shut and the 4:45 was a length out, turning.

A taxi, then, around the water. The driver understood the hour on my ticket and drove to it—the shoulder where the lane ran out, an amber taken late. It was not enough. The shore road held its evening, the same as every evening, and the water sat there uncrossed to my right the whole way.

The desk at the counter still had someone behind it. The 7:45 had gone twenty minutes back; the board had already dropped it. The next thing to reach the city left at 4:15 and landed at 6:04, first light on the far side.

First light was when the machines came, and when my mother would take her place at the corner.

Board the 4:15.

Return home.
