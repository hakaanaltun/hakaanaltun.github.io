The coast fell away below the wing.

Nothing came in the window but the cabin and, past it, the dark. The flat came instead.

His father, and the measuring. Once a year for a run of years his father stood him against the doorframe and laid a hand flat on the crown of his head to take the hair out of the count, and made a pencil mark where the hand had been, and wrote the year beside it small, and each mark came higher than the last, up toward his father’s hand—and that hand had been a year gone now, and the last mark stood level with his own shoulder, and by first light the frame and the marks on it would be down in the street.

A holiday morning once, up before it was light, the new clothes stiff over the chair since the night before. The first leaving after that, for the other city and the university, a bag heavier than this one—his mother at the door, and a hundred metres on he turned to check, and she was at the window, and he raised an arm, and she raised hers.

The first absence had a lie folded in it. Midterms done and the university shut a fortnight, he told home a week—exams still to sit, back straight after—and gave the free week to that city and to nothing he could name later. When he came, his mother and his father sat quiet at the table over a breakfast that would not lift. His grandfather had been a week in the ground. They had kept it from him: he had exams, they said, and no sense in his sitting them in mourning.

The not-being-there ran shorter and came faster. A call taken in a corridor, a date that could not be moved. The last of them needed no lie and left no one to spare him—a ward reached the day after, the bed already stripped.

All of it in the time the seatbelt light took to go dark.

Then the announcement, and the descent.
