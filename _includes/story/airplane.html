The coast fell away below the wing.

Nothing came in the window but the cabin and, past it, the dark. What came instead came from the flat.

The doorframe, and the measuring. Once a year for a run of years a hand had stood a child to the jamb and laid itself flat to the crown to take the hair out of the count, and marked the pencil where the hand had been, and written the year beside it small, and each mark had come higher than the last, up toward the hand that set them—and the hand had been a year gone, and the last mark stood level with a shoulder now, and by first light the jamb and the marks and the wall behind would be down in the street.

A holiday morning once, up before it was light, the new clothes stiff over the chair since the night before. The first leaving after that, for the other city and the university, a bag heavier than this one—the mother at the door, and a hundred metres on the turn to check, and she was at the window, and an arm went up, and hers.

The not-being-there ran shorter and came faster. A call taken in a corridor. A date that could not be moved. The last of them a ward reached the day after, when the reaching was already only paperwork, and the bed was stripped.

All of it in the time the seatbelt light took to go dark.

Then the announcement, and the descent.
