The coast fell away below the wing.

Nothing came in the window but the cabin and, past it, the dark. The flat came instead.

My father, and the measuring. Once a year for a run of years he stood me to the doorframe and laid his hand flat to my crown to take the hair out of the count, and marked the pencil where his hand had been, and wrote the year beside it small, and each mark came higher than the last, up toward the hand that set them—and the hand had been a year gone now, and the last mark stood level with my shoulder, and by first light the frame and the marks on it would be down in the street.

A holiday morning once, up before it was light, the new clothes stiff over the chair since the night before. The first leaving after that, for the other city and the university, a bag heavier than this one—my mother at the door, and a hundred metres on I turned to check, and she was at the window, and I raised an arm, and she raised hers.

The first absence had a lie folded in it. Midterms done and the university shut a fortnight, I told home a week—exams still to sit, back straight after—and gave the free week to that city and to nothing I could name later. When I came, my mother and my father sat quiet at the table over a breakfast that would not lift. My grandfather had been a week in the ground. They had kept it from me: I had exams, they said, and no sense in my sitting them in mourning.

The not-being-there ran shorter and came faster. A call taken in a corridor, a date that could not be moved. The last of them needed no lie and left no one to spare me—a ward reached the day after, the bed already stripped.

All of it in the time the seatbelt light took to go dark.

Then the announcement, and the descent.
