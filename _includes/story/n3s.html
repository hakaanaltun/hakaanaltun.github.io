The doors of the 4:45 closed on the minute. Across the water the airport came on light by light, and the last of the day went with the crossing. The wake ran white behind, and was a long time settling.

The gate, then a seat over the wing.

The runway came up in the dark, and the city after it, strung along the water.

The building where my mother lived now stood back from a newer road; the lift worked, and her name was typed on a card by the door. Dinner was already on when I rang. We ate at the small table, the television low in the other room for a company neither of us watched.

The block was not mentioned. The door on it stayed shut.

Somewhere in the talk the old thing came up again—the photograph of the two of us, gone in the moves years back and never found; without it the home was only in my head now. She had heard it before and let it pass again, and passed the bread.

The evening did not lift. It stayed at the table after the plates were cleared. It sat on my chest through the tea, and was still there when the light in the small room went out.

Morning.
