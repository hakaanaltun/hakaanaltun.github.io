The doors of the 4:45 closed on the minute. Across the water the airport came on light by light, and the last of the day went with the crossing. The wake ran white behind, and was a long time settling.

The runway came up in the dark, and the city after it, strung along the water.

The building where the mother lived now stood back from a newer road; the lift worked, and her name was typed on a card by the door. Dinner was already on when the bell went. It was eaten at the small table, the television low in the other room for a company neither of them watched.

The block was not mentioned. It sat in the flat like a guest put in the next room to wait, and both of them kept the door on it shut.

Somewhere in the talk the old thing came up again, as it always did—that the home was only in the head now, that it had gone in the move years back and no amount of looking had turned it up. The mother had heard it before and let it pass again, and passed the bread.

The evening did not lift. It stayed at the table after the plates were cleared. It sat on the chest through the tea, and was still there when the light in the small room went out.

Morning.
