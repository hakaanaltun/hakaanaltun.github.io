The doors of the 4:45 closed on the minute. Across the water the airport came on light by light, and the last of the day went with the crossing. The wake ran white behind, and was a long time settling.

The runway came up in the dark, and the city after it, strung along the water.

The building where his mother lived now stood back from a newer road; the lift worked, and her name was typed on a card by the door. Dinner was already on when he rang. They ate at the small table, the television low in the other room for a company neither of them watched.

The block was not mentioned. The door on it stayed shut.

She talked instead of Germany—the factory line she had worked, the money sent home—and of the leaving, settled between the two families while she was still there. She had come back a bride to a door she had never seen, and stayed behind it forty years.

One story had his father in it. A shopping day, the car full, his father in the front right seat, his mother and her sister behind. No one had yet said in his hearing which sister was his. He rode the whole way hoping it was her. He had told her that years into the marriage, and she told it now, at the small table.

Somewhere in the talk the old thing came up again—the photograph of the two of them, gone in the moves years back and never found; without it the home was only in his head now. She had heard it before and let it pass again, and passed the bread.

The evening did not lift. It stayed at the table after the plates were cleared. It sat on his chest through the tea, and was still there when the light in the small room went out.
